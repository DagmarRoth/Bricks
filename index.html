Running Into Brick

How a preventable injury epidemic affects American children

Every year, thousands of American children visit emergency rooms and hospitals after colliding with brick. Running through homes, playing in schools, walking in parks—in moments of inattention or misstep, brick structures exact a toll. This analysis of national hospital data reveals the surprising patterns of injury, the children most at risk, and the persistent danger that hard surfaces pose to developing bodies.

The Scale of an Unseen Problem

The National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (NEISS) tracks all emergency room visits related to product and environmental injuries across a representative sample of U.S. hospitals. In a single year, the data reveals thousands of hospital visits by children under 18 involving brick-related injuries—a quiet epidemic that remains largely unexamined.

Key Finding: Brick-related injuries are not rare accidents. They form a consistent pattern of preventable trauma, concentrated among children whose developmental stage makes them particularly vulnerable.

Who Gets Hurt: Age and Vulnerability

Brick injuries are not randomly distributed across childhood. Instead, they cluster in a narrow window— the ages when children are most physically active, least coordinated, and most likely to be unsupervised in environments with hard surfaces.

Children ages 6-12 experience the highest rate of brick-related injuries. The peak occurs during elementary school years, when physical activity is highest and spatial awareness is still developing.

The pattern is clear: 40% of all brick injuries occur in children ages 6-12. This is precisely the age group least equipped to predict or avoid obstacles, most likely to be engaged in unsupervised play, and most prone to miscalculating distances and speeds.

The Anatomy of Injury

Brick is unforgiving. When children collide with it, they collide with their most vulnerable features first. The data speaks to a troubling pattern of facial and cranial trauma.

Head injuries represent the single largest category of brick-related trauma in children. Lacerations to the face and scalp are nearly as common. Together, injuries above the neck account for a disproportionate share of all cases.

Why this matters: Head injuries carry the highest risk of lasting neurological consequences. Even "minor" lacerations to the face can require emergency stitching and leave permanent scars. The prevalence of head trauma in brick collisions is not coincidental—it reflects the physics of how children fall and the locations where bricks are most densely present.

Where Bricks Hide: The Geography of Risk

Brick injuries don't happen in isolation. They cluster in specific environments—places where children live, learn, and play, and where brick surfaces are most common.

Home emerges as the single most dangerous environment, accounting for more brick injuries than schools and recreational areas combined. Fireplaces, interior walls, and porch structures present hazards that families rarely consider.

This finding challenges common assumptions about where children get hurt. While schools and playgrounds are often the focus of safety regulations, the family home presents the greatest brick-related risk. The brick fireplace, the decorative wall, the porch column—these familiar structures within the intimate spaces of family life pose a consistent threat.

The Nature of Trauma

What exactly happens to a child's body when it meets brick at speed? The injury patterns tell a story of impact trauma—sudden, violent, and often requiring immediate medical intervention.

Lacerations dominate the injury landscape, followed by contusions and abrasions. These are not minor scrapes—they are significant wounds requiring stitches, wound care, and ongoing medical monitoring.

Medical reality: While lacerations are survivable, they carry risks of infection, permanent scarring, and disfigurement. Many of these injuries to children's faces will leave lifelong marks.

Severity: Age Paradox

An unexpected pattern emerges from the data: while younger children sustain more brick injuries overall, older children are more likely to require hospitalization. This suggests that the injuries older children sustain are more severe—a function perhaps of the greater speed and force involved in their collisions.

The relationship between age, injury frequency, and severity reveals a complex picture. Children ages 6-10 sustain the most injuries, but children ages 11-18 face higher hospitalization rates, indicating more serious outcomes.

What This Means

Brick-related injuries in children are not random accidents—they are foreseeable consequences of environments that combine hard surfaces with spaces where children naturally move and play.

The data suggests that tens of thousands of preventable injuries occur each year because families, schools, and public spaces have not adequately assessed the risk that brick structures pose to children. A fireplace without protective padding, a brick wall in a high-traffic area, a hard brick bench in a playground—these are not inevitable hazards. They are design choices.

For parents and educators, the numbers should prompt a simple action: audit the brick surfaces in spaces where children spend time. For architects and designers, they should prompt a fundamental question: does brick belong in child-occupied spaces without protective measures?

Methodology

Data comes from the National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (NEISS), operated by the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission. NEISS tracks a nationally representative sample of approximately 100 emergency departments across the United States.

This analysis includes all hospital visits where the injury narrative mentioned brick or brick structures, filtered to children under age 18. Cases span a full year of data collection and are weighted to represent national estimates.
