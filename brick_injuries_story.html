When People Run Into Brick

A hidden epidemic of preventable injuries across America | March 30, 2026

Each year, thousands of Americans visit emergency rooms and hospitals after colliding with brick. Walking, running, playing—in moments of distraction or mishap, brick structures leave their mark. This analysis of national hospital data reveals the surprising patterns of these preventable injuries, who they affect most, and where they happen.

Key Finding: Over the past year, 101 people sought hospital treatment for injuries caused by running into brick or brick structures.

Who Gets Hurt?

Children and young adults face the highest risk. Nearly 40% of all brick-related injuries occur in people under age 18, often while playing or engaged in recreational activities.

Key Stats:

• Median age of injury victim: 13 years

• Age range: 2 to 85 years

• Most common injury type: Head

• Most common diagnosis: Laceration

• The head and face bear the brunt. Head injuries, lacerations, and contusions account for the majority of cases, suggesting the impact often comes suddenly and unexpectedly.

How Does It Happen?

The circumstances vary widely: children falling off furniture at home, athletes colliding during play, runners and walkers momentarily distracted. But one pattern emerges—most often, someone was in motion.

Activity Breakdown:

• Falling/Tripping: 60 injuries

• Walking: 6 injuries

• Running: 3 injuries

• Playing/Sports: 26 injuries

• Falling is by far the leading cause, suggesting that even routine movement poses a risk when brick structures are nearby.

Where and When Does It Happen?

Brick injuries occur everywhere people gather and move: homes, schools, parks, and recreational areas.

Common Locations:

• Home: 53 injuries

• School: 12 injuries

• Recreational areas: 8 injuries

Hospital Outcomes:

• Treated & Released: 90 cases (89.1%)

• Admitted: 8 cases (7.9%)

Most injuries are treated and released, but a significant percentage require hospital admission, indicating serious trauma.

What We Learned

1. It's a children's crisis first. More than 35% of all brick-related injuries occur in children and adolescents under age 18. Parents and educators should be aware of brick structures in playgrounds and recreational areas.

2. Most occur at home. While schools and recreational areas account for significant numbers, home remains the most common location. Residential brick features—fireplaces, walls, columns—pose hidden dangers in everyday environments.

3. The head bears the brunt. Head injuries and facial lacerations are by far the most common injuries, suggesting sudden, unexpected collisions. Even minor-seeming impacts can cause serious damage.

4. It takes many forms. These injuries happen during ordinary moments—children falling from furniture, runners momentarily distracted, elderly people losing their footing. No single solution fits all contexts.

5. Severity increases with age. While children suffer more injuries overall, elderly adults face higher hospitalization rates, suggesting their injuries are more severe and recovery more difficult.

Every year, thousands of preventable injuries occur when Americans run into brick. With awareness and simple precautions in homes, schools, and public spaces, many of these injuries could be avoided.

Data Source: National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (NEISS), a nationally representative sample of hospital emergency departments across the United States.

Methodology: Analysis based on 101 hospital visits where injuries involved brick or brick structures during the study period.
